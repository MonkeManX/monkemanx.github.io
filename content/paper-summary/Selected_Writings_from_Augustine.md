This summary and critic was created in the context of the seminar “Introduction to Saint Augustine,” which I attended at the *Karlsruhe Institute of Technology*. It is not a summary of a single work, but rather a summary of selected chapters from various works by him, such as *The City of God* or *Confession*.

## Background

Augustine of Hippo (354–430) was a Christian bishop in North Africa, one of the most influential thinkers of late antiquity. His major work, The City of God, was written in the aftermath of the Sack of Rome (410), when Rome was captured for the first time in centuries during the migrations of various peoples.

This event led to widespread criticism of Christianity. Many pagans argued that Rome had prospered under the traditional Roman gods, but after turning to Christianity, the empire had declined and suffered disaster.

Augustine wrote The City of God as a response to these accusations. The work serves both as a defense of Christianity and as a broader philosophical and theological interpretation of history, arguing that earthly cities like Rome are ultimately transient, while the “City of God” is eternal and grounded in divine truth.

## City of God, Book I, Chapter 1-2 and 7-15: Human Suffering in Gods World

In the present text, Augustine of Hippo explains why suffering exists, even for those who are Christians. To do this, he examines various forms of suffering that occurred during the destruction of Rome and argues why these are not actually so bad. I will first present these positions before offering criticism.

He begins by saying that many who criticize Christianity owe their survival to it. This happened, he claims, because the barbarians showed mercy out of respect for Christ (DCD I,1). He further explains that looting and killing are normal in war, whereas sparing churches is rather unusual, and that this mercy should be attributed not to the barbarians themselves but to Christ (DCD I,2;7).

Next, he addresses the question of why suffering befalls both good and bad people. He argues that there are several reasons:

* It allows evil people to repent and improve.
* Suffering has different functions: it strengthens good people (training in virtue), while it corrupts bad people (making them even worse).
* Suffering and prosperity are distributed among both good and bad people so that external prosperity is not mistaken for the true good (DCD I,8).
* Another reason is that suffering tests faith, and that even good people commit minor sins (DCD I,9).

In Chapter 10, he tries to comfort Christians by saying that they lost nothing truly valuable in the sack of Rome, since wealth and material goods are not essential—spiritual goods are. Moreover, loss helps detach people from material things. He also argues that even the death of Christians during the sack is not bad, since all people must die, and what matters is how one dies and what comes afterward (DCD I,11). A lack of burial is also not a problem, since funerals are primarily for the living (DCD I,13), and God will resurrect people regardless of the state of their bodies (DCD I,12). Finally, he claims that even captivity or slavery after the sack is not so terrible, since God is with people everywhere (DCD I,14–15).

I find that this text has several argumentative weaknesses. Chapters 1, 2, and 7 seem more like anecdotes than rigorous arguments. Augustine tells a vague story in which many people were spared in one place and not in another, and attributes this to God. The problem is that this account remains very unspecific: it lacks precise numbers or systematic comparisons. Even if his account is correct, there are many possible alternative explanations for why people were spared that have nothing to do with Christianity. Augustine would either need to refute these alternatives—which he does not—or provide a convincing argument for why Christ in particular is the cause, which is also missing.

Furthermore, in Chapter 9 he argues that one reason for the suffering of good people is that they, too, sin and therefore deserve punishment or purification. However, he does not consider the scale of suffering: if his claim were true, one would expect good people to experience less or milder suffering. In reality, suffering (such as illness or accidents) appears to be distributed largely independently of moral character.

Another issue is whether such great suffering is necessary at all to achieve the goals he names (e.g., detachment from material goods in Chapter 10). It seems plausible that such insights could be conveyed through much lesser suffering. For example, one can teach a child to stop at a red light without them first having to be hit by a car.


## City of God, Book I, Chapters 16-22, 24-29, 31, 33-35: Human Suffering in Gods World

In Book I of *City of God* (chapters 16–22, 24–29, 31, 33–35), Augustine of Hippo develops a sustained argument about virtue, the soul, violence, and especially the moral status of suicide in response to the trauma of Rome’s sack.

A central theme in chapters 16–22 is that moral worth lies in the soul, not the body. Augustine argues that acts such as rape, though physically violating, do not corrupt a person’s inner virtue if their will remains pure. Purity is thus located in intention rather than bodily condition. From this, he concludes that suicide in response to rape is unjustified: the victim has not sinned and therefore should not punish themselves. In discussing examples from Roman history, he insists that even if society judges a victim harshly, divine judgment, grounded in truth, matters more than public opinion.

This leads into his broader argument against suicide. Augustine claims that the commandment “Thou shalt not kill” is absolute and includes killing oneself. Suicide is wrong because it involves usurping judgment that belongs only to legitimate authority, ultimately God. While killing plants and animals is permitted (since they lack rationality), human life is inviolable due to its rational nature. However, in chapter 21 he introduces apparent exceptions: killing may be justified if commanded by law or directly by God, such as in war or biblical narratives.

In chapter 22, Augustine rejects the idea that suicide could ever be virtuous. Even if motivated by fear of sin or a desire to preserve purity, it remains morally wrong. He argues that committing a certain sin (suicide) to avoid a possible future sin is irrational, especially since after death no repentance is possible. This idea is developed further in chapter 25, where he contends that one should prefer an uncertain future (where sin might occur) over a guaranteed sin that eliminates the possibility of repentance.

In chapters 24 and 26, Augustine considers examples of individuals who maintained virtue under extreme conditions or who took their own lives to avoid dishonor. While some of these figures were revered, Augustine cautiously suggests that such actions might only be justified if directly commanded by God—something not generally accessible to ordinary people. Thus, suicide remains broadly prohibited.

Chapter 27 addresses the argument that one might commit suicide after repenting, thereby avoiding future sin. Augustine dismisses this as a kind of madness, asserting that it misunderstands the nature of moral life. In chapter 28, he reflects on why God might allow the violation of virtuous individuals: possible reasons include humbling the proud, preventing future arrogance, or correcting misunderstandings about virtue—emphasizing again that true virtue resides in the soul, not the body.

Later chapters (29, 33–35) broaden the scope. Augustine contrasts Christian and pagan interpretations of disaster, arguing that Christians understand suffering as meaningful within divine providence, while pagans lack such a framework. He also claims that Rome’s survival and the refuge found in Christian spaces demonstrate God’s influence, even as he criticizes ongoing moral corruption among the Romans.

Despite the coherence of Augustine’s framework, several philosophical problems remain unresolved.

First, in chapter 20, Augustine grounds the prohibition against killing in the concept of rationality, claiming that humans, unlike animals or plants, possess a rational nature that grants them moral status. However, he does not explain why rationality is the निर्णing criterion for moral worth. What exactly constitutes rationality, and why should its absence justify domination or killing? This raises further tension with historical practices such as slavery, widely accepted in Augustine’s time and present in biblical contexts, since enslaved humans are clearly rational beings. If rationality grounds moral inviolability, the justification for treating some humans as property becomes deeply problematic.

Second, in chapter 21, Augustine introduces an apparent contradiction. He initially presents the commandment “Thou shalt not kill” as absolute, yet later allows exceptions when killing is sanctioned by human law or commanded by God. The appeal to divine command is internally consistent within his theology, but the inclusion of human law is more troubling. If divine law is supreme and absolute, it is unclear why or how human legal systems could legitimately override it. This seems to undermine the claimed absoluteness of the commandment.

Third, Augustine’s argument about suicide and rationality raises difficult edge cases. If the prohibition on killing is grounded in rational nature, what happens when a person loses that rationality, for example, in advanced stages of illnesses like Alzheimer's disease? Augustine does not address whether such individuals retain the same moral status or whether his framework would imply different conclusions about self-killing in such circumstances.

Finally, in chapter 27, Augustine dismisses the argument that suicide could prevent future sin by calling it “madness,” but he does not fully engage with its underlying logic. If one accepts his premise that sin is the greatest evil and that repentance is necessary for salvation, then the idea of eliminating future sin through death, after repentance, seems to require a more substantial rebuttal. Simply labeling the position as irrational leaves a gap in the argument, suggesting that a deeper justification is missing.


## Confessiones VII.viii.10 – VII.xiii.19: Nature of God


In Chapter 10, Confessions discusses why astrology cannot be true. He argues that twins are often born at the same moment under the same stars, yet they live very different lives and experience different fates. This shows that human destiny cannot be determined by the stars. Augustine explains that when astrologers sometimes seem correct, it is not because astrology is true, but because they are able to perceive things already present in a person’s soul, and ultimately this depends on God’s providence rather than celestial forces.

In Chapter 11, Augustine reflects on the unchanging nature of God and begins to connect this with the idea of life after death. He also wrestles deeply with the problem of evil. At this stage, Augustine describes an inner conflict within himself: although he knows he should value inward and spiritual things, he remains attached to external and worldly concerns.

In Chapter 12, Augustine explains that he became certain of God’s existence by turning away from external things and looking inward. Through inward reflection he discovered truths that surpassed the material world and pointed him toward God.

In Chapter 13, Augustine compares the teachings of the Platonists with the opening of the Gospel of John. He notices that both speak about the “Word” and that the Word is divine. Augustine is struck by the similarity between Platonic philosophy and Christian theology, especially in their understanding of a higher divine reality.

In Chapter 14, Augustine discusses the nature of Christ. The Son, Jesus Christ, is born not from human substance but from the substance of God. Yet Christ also takes on human form through an act of divine humility or self-emptying. Augustine emphasizes that Christ existed before time itself and later died for humanity’s sins, especially for the impious and sinful.

In Chapter 15, Augustine notes that both Christianity and the Platonists agree that God is incorruptible and unchanging. However, this raises a question for him: if God is incorruptible, why does Scripture describe corruption and idolatry in Genesis and in human history? Augustine is trying to reconcile the perfection of God with the evident corruption present in the world.

In Chapter 16, Augustine explains that he first came closer to God not through the Bible but through Platonic books. By looking inward, he experienced a vision of God that transcended his own being. He realized that God is superior because God is true being itself, whereas Augustine, as a created being, only exists through dependence on God. This insight humbles him and transforms his understanding of existence.

In Chapter 17, Augustine argues that everything below God lacks absolute being because all created things come from God and are changeable. Only God possesses absolute being because only God is completely unchanging. Created things exist, but their existence is imperfect and dependent.

In Chapter 18, Augustine develops his theory of evil. The supreme good, God, cannot be corrupted. However, corruption can only occur in things that are already good, because corruption is the loss or privation of some good. Something completely without goodness would not exist at all. Therefore, everything that exists must possess some goodness simply because it exists. Augustine concludes that evil is not a substance in itself, because if evil were a substance it would also possess goodness. Instead, evil is the privation or absence of goodness. Since all things are created by God, all created things are fundamentally good.

In Chapter 19, Augustine concludes that evil does not truly exist for God, nor for the universe as a whole, because nothing exists outside God’s creation. What humans perceive as evil often arises from conflicts of interest between different parts of creation. Yet every part of the universe contributes to the goodness of the whole. Therefore, all things should ultimately be praised as parts of God’s ordered creation. Augustine also argues that humans should not wish for individual things to become better in isolation, because the totality of creation together already forms a greater and more perfect good.
